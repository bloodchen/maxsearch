Why do DNS failures happen?

Exploring why your browser can't find a website's address, from local configuration errors to global DNS server outages.

  • The browser shows 'DNS_PROBE_FINISHED_NXDOMAIN' or 'Server Not Found'

  • The internet connection is active, but web addresses won't resolve
  • Changing networks (e.g., from Wi-Fi to Data) suddenly fixes the issue

‚úÖ Short answer

A DNS failure means your browser can't translate a website's name (like example.com) into its numerical IP address. This is usually due to a temporary glitch in your ISP's servers, an incorrect setting on your device, or the website itself hasn't updated its records correctly.

  • Your device is asking a DNS 'phonebook' for an address, but the phonebook is missing or unreachable
  • Corrupted local DNS cache is providing outdated or incorrect information
  • Your Router or ISP's DNS servers are experiencing high traffic or downtime
  • A firewall or security software is blocking the DNS lookup process
  • The website owner recently changed servers and the update hasn't reached you yet

üß† Why this behavior exists

  • DNS is a distributed system, meaning data must travel across many servers to reach you
  • ISP DNS servers can become overloaded during peak usage times
  • Misconfigured network settings (like static IPs) can point to non-existent DNS servers
  • Public Wi-Fi networks often have restrictive DNS settings that cause timeouts

  • Restarting the browser won't fix a system-level DNS configuration issue

  • Updating your browser won't resolve a network-wide DNS failure

‚úîÔ∏è What you can and cannot do

What you can do

  • Flush your DNS cache (e.g., 'ipconfig /flushdns' on Windows or similar on Mac)
  • Switch to a reliable public DNS provider like Google (8.8.8.8) or Cloudflare (1.1.1.1)
  • Restart your router to clear its internal DNS cache and renew the connection
  • Check if the site is actually down using an 'Is It Down' service
  • Disable VPNs or Proxies temporarily to see if they are interfering with DNS

What you cannot do

  • Force a website owner to fix their broken DNS records
  • Improve the speed of the global DNS propagation process
  • Access a site if the domain registration has expired
  • Fix a major ISP backbone outage that affects DNS routing

üìå Scope and applicability

  • Affects all web-connected applications, not just your browser
  • Can be specific to one device or an entire local network
  • Often resolved automatically as DNS records propagate
  • Depends on both your local settings and external server health
